Why this matters

Taiwan should not only be a market for AI — it should help define how AI hears its language, voice, and culture.

Today's large models are trained mostly on English, Simplified Chinese, generic Mandarin, and global web text. They can produce Traditional Chinese, but they do not necessarily understand Taiwan: its public-document tone, institutions, place names, history, Taiwanese Mandarin, Taigi and Hakka accents, Bopomofo, Tailo, and the real sound of Taiwan's meetings, classrooms, and local life. OpenFormosa exists to close that gap.

Where today's AI still misses Taiwan.

Generic models can write Traditional Chinese, yet five gaps show up again and again in real Taiwan use.

01

Imprecise cultural context

Terms, institutions, place names, and history get swapped for non-Taiwan versions, from health insurance to local government.

02

Speech that isn't local

Taiwan Mandarin, Taigi/Hakka accents, code-switching, meetings, classes, and night-market audio differ from standard Mandarin training sets.

03

TTS without Taiwan tone

Many voices speak Chinese but not natural Taiwanese Mandarin, and mishandle Bopomofo, Tailo, polyphones, place names, and personal names.

04

Documents left behind

Official letters, invoices, receipts, forms, menus, temple lots, and Taiwan address formats need OCR and structure that generic models skip.

05

Data sovereignty and cost

Enterprises, schools, and the public sector cannot ship sensitive data to overseas APIs. Taiwan needs private-deployable, auditable, affordable models.

Open is a trust mechanism

We open-source more than models.

Open source is how we earn trust. Beyond model weights, we build Taiwan-local evaluation and benchmark systems, publish the model cards and lessons learned from training, and open the core wherever we can.

Read evaluation workflow
  • Open models. Deployable, fine-tunable weights and specs for the Barbet, BlackBear, BlueMagpie, and SikaDeer family.
  • Open evaluation systems. Taiwan-local benchmarks, scoring scripts, and leaderboards, so capability can actually be tested.
  • Model cards and lessons. Training recipes, tokenizer notes, checkpoints, limitations, and the experience behind each run.
  • Open the core whenever we can. Code, tokenizer specs, benchmark tools, and safe summaries should be public whenever possible.
